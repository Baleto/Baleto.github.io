Cookies in Websites

HTTP cookies are small name‚Äìvalue pairs stored by the browser. They allow sites to implement sessions, remember preferences, measure analytics, and more. The table below contrasts common categories of cookies.

Category Purpose Examples Consent? Typical Duration
Strictly Necessary Core site functionality (auth sessions, CSRF protection, load balancing). sessionid, csrftoken, routing/sticky cookies. Generally not consent-gated (but still documented). Session or short-term.
Performance Improves speed and reliability (CDN metrics, error tracking). Real-user monitoring beacons, crash IDs. Often consent-gated depending on jurisdiction. Short to medium.
Functional Remember user choices (language, theme, accessibility settings). theme=dark, locale, UI preferences. Usually requires consent or explicit user action. Medium to long.
Analytics Aggregated usage measurement and engagement statistics. Analytics IDs (e.g., GA), first-party analytics cookies. Consent required in many regions (e.g., EU). Medium to long.

Summary. It is best to keep strictly necessary cookies minimal and clearly documented. Non-essential cookies (performance, functional, analytics) should be gated behind consent where required by law, with easy ways for users to withdraw that consent. Favor first-party, short-lived identifiers and clear retention policies to respect both privacy and user expectations.
